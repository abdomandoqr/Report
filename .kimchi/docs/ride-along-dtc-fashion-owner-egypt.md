# A Day in the Shoes of a DTC Fashion Store Owner in Egypt

## 6:47 AM – Still in bed, already panicking

The alarm was supposed to go off at 7:15, but I’m already awake. My phone screen is glowing with WhatsApp notifications — 23 unread messages, three of them from customers who ordered last night and want to know if we shipped yet. One of them is typing. I can see the three dots.

I scroll past a message from Bosta saying a package was returned. Again. Thirty-seventh street, Nasr City. Wrong address, even though the customer swore it was correct. That’s 42 EGP in shipping we’ll never get back. Plus the customer is furious, asking why we didn’t call before dispatch.

*Thinking: "We did call. We WhatsApped. She read it and didn’t reply. Now it’s our fault. There goes another 42 EGP. That’s maybe the fifth return this week just from bad addresses."*

I check the Shopify dashboard before my feet hit the floor. Overnight: 31 orders. Nineteen of them are COD. The Meta Ads are still running — I can see the spend already crossed 1,200 EGP and it’s not even 7 AM. ROAS yesterday was 3.2×. Not bad, but last month it was 4.8×. The creative is dying. I can feel it.

I get up. No coffee yet. My mom’s already in the kitchen, heating foul. I mumble something about being late and grab my laptop bag.

---

## 8:15 AM – The WhatsApp avalanche begins

I’m at the small office — really just a converted apartment in Heliopolis — and Mariam, my only full-time employee, is already on her second cup of tea. She handles customer service, or tries to. Her personal WhatsApp has 847 unread messages. She had to reset it three times last month because it crashed.

**Mariam:** "Boss, we have a problem. The black oversized shirt in medium? We sold twelve yesterday but the sheet says we only have nine."

I open the Shopify inventory. It says 9. But I know we have a box in the corner from last week’s delivery that hasn’t been scanned in yet. Or maybe we do — I can’t remember if those mediums were actually in that box, or if those were the smalls the factory messed up.

*Thinking: "This is the third time this month. Marketing is pushing products that don’t exist, and I’m the one who has to call customers and offer a refund or a different size. Each refund takes twenty minutes of WhatsApp negotiation. And every refund is a customer who probably won’t come back."*

I tell Mariam to hold the three unverified orders until we do a physical count. Then I check COD confirmations. We manually WhatsApp every COD customer within two hours of order. Industry standard, supposedly. But we only got through fourteen of last night’s nineteen before midnight. Five are still unconfirmed.

One of the unconfirmed orders is for 1,850 EGP. Three items. First-time customer. New phone number. Red flag, but we need the sale.

*Thinking: "If I ship it and it’s fake, that’s 60 EGP in round-trip shipping. If I don’t ship it and it’s real, I lose 1,850 EGP in revenue. I have no data to make this decision. I’m guessing. Every single time, I’m guessing."*

---

## 9:30 AM – Meta Ads meeting that should have been an email

Rami, my freelance media buyer, calls on Zoom. He’s in Alexandria, working from a café. His background is a blurred beach photo. I’m jealous.

**Rami:** "The ROAS on the Ramadan creative is dropping. We were at 4.1× last week. It’s 2.7× now."

"Creative fatigue?" I ask, already knowing the answer.

"Definitely. Egyptian audiences burn through creative fast. The same video that killed it two weeks ago is now expensive."

He wants to shoot new content. Model, photographer, location, styling. Minimum 8,000 EGP for a half-day. Or we can use stock UGC from a creator marketplace — cheaper, but every other brand in Cairo is using the same girl in the same abaya.

*Thinking: "Eight thousand EGP for content that might last ten days before it burns. That’s almost as much as I pay Mariam in a month. But if I don’t do it, ROAS keeps dropping, and the algorithm starts punishing the ad account. It’s a treadmill. A very expensive treadmill."*

We agree to test two new videos by Thursday. I hang up and immediately transfer 4,000 EGP to the photographer as a deposit. Meta will eat another 2,000 EGP today whether I like it or not.

---

## 10:45 AM – Factory drama, again

I call Mahmoud at the factory in Obour. He answers on the third ring, sounding like he’s in a wind tunnel.

"The shipment for the summer drop," I say. "You promised Thursday."

"Inshallah, next Tuesday," he says.

"You said Thursday."

"The dyeing took longer. The color came out wrong on the first batch. We had to redo it."

This is standard. Obour factory time is not real time. Every season, they delay by four to seven days. And every season, I plan for it — but then the marketing team (which is me) announces the drop date to customers, and the factory misses it, and I spend three days apologizing on Instagram Stories.

*Thinking: "I should find a new factory. But every other factory owner says the same things. At least Mahmoud’s quality is decent. The last factory I tried sent me stitching that fell apart after two washes. That cost me 12,000 EGP in refunds and a viral complaint video on TikTok. I’m stuck with Mahmoud."*

I update the production sheet in a Google Doc that Mariam and I both edit but never quite trust. I move the drop date to the following Sunday. Then I message our Instagram followers with a vague "something exciting is coming soon" Story to buy time.

---

## 12:15 PM – Lunch at the desk, if you can call it that

Mariam brought koshary from the place downstairs. We eat at the packing station because there’s no kitchen table. While we eat, I try to reconcile yesterday’s COD cash.

Bosta says they delivered 31 packages and collected 14,200 EGP. My Shopify says 33 COD orders went out. Two are unaccounted for. I check the Bosta dashboard — one shows "rescheduled delivery," the other says "customer not available." Both will probably come back tomorrow. Both still cost me shipping.

MylerZ, which handles Alexandria and the coast, delivered 8 orders and collected 3,100 EGP. But their payout won’t hit my bank until next Wednesday. Bosta pays faster — usually two days — but they’re more expensive per delivery.

*Thinking: "So yesterday’s ‘revenue’ was somewhere between 14,200 and 17,300 EGP. But I won’t actually know until next week. And the Meta bill for yesterday alone was 2,400 EGP. Product cost is roughly 35%. Shipping average is 28 EGP per order. Returns are running at 22%. I think I made profit yesterday. I genuinely don’t know."*

Mariam asks if she should post the new arrival on Instagram. I say yes, but don’t tag the price until we confirm inventory. We’ve tagged prices before on sold-out items and then spent the afternoon DMing apologies.

---

## 1:30 PM – The size-chart crisis

A customer messages on WhatsApp: "I’m 75 kg and 165 cm. What size should I get?"

This is the most common message we get. We have a size chart. It’s in the product photos. It lists bust, waist, length in centimeters. Nobody reads it. They want a human to tell them.

Mariam replies, guessing based on experience. The customer orders an XL. Two days later, she’ll message saying it’s too big and she wants to exchange. We’ll send a courier to collect it — another 35 EGP — and ship a replacement — another 35 EGP. We’ll lose 70 EGP on the exchange, but if we say no, she’ll post a Story about how our sizing sucks.

*Thinking: "Sizing returns cost us at least 4,000 EGP per month. Maybe more. I don’t even track it properly. There’s no category in Shopify for ‘exchange shipping loss.’ It just disappears into the courier bills."*

While Mariam handles the size inquiry, I try to update the Excel sheet where I track ad spend against revenue. I get three columns in before a notification pops up: Shopify fraud analysis flagged an order. Same name, different phone, shipping to a new address. Total: 2,300 EGP. Fraud score: medium.

Shopify fraud analysis is basically useless in Egypt. It has no idea how Egyptian phone numbers work, no idea which governorates are risky, no idea that a first-time customer ordering 2,300 EGP at 1 AM from Sohag is basically guaranteed to RTO.

I approve it anyway. Because declining real orders hurts worse than shipping fake ones.

*Thinking: "I’m subsidizing the country’s fake-order habit one shipment at a time."*

---

## 3:00 PM – The courier argument

Bosta calls. Their account manager, Ahmed, is a nice guy but he has bad news every time.

**Ahmed:** "Your return rate in Upper Egypt is 38%. We need to talk about a surcharge for Minya and Sohag."

"That’s not our fault," I say. "Your drivers don’t call before they arrive. Customers aren’t home."

"We do call. They don’t answer. Or they say ‘come tomorrow.’ Then they cancel."

We go back and forth for ten minutes. The truth is, nobody knows whose fault it is. Bosta blames customers. Customers blame Bosta. I’m in the middle, paying for both sides.

We settle on a temporary rate increase of 3 EGP per delivery for governorates with >30% return rates. On 150 deliveries per week, that’s 450 EGP extra. Just because people in Minya change their minds.

*Thinking: "I could stop shipping to Minya. But that’s 15% of our orders. The margins in Cairo are better, but the volume in Upper Egypt is real. Every founder I know has this exact same argument with their courier every quarter. It’s part of the business model now — baked-in inefficiency."*

After the call, I open the Dukkan dashboard just to see what I’d be paying if I switched platforms. Dukkan has better local integrations, but migrating 400 products would take weeks I don’t have.

---

## 4:45 PM – The TikTok that almost broke us

Our TikTok creator sent the draft for the live-shopping clip. She’s a micro-influencer in Alexandria with 45K followers. She’s wearing our beige linen set, dancing to a Mahragan track. It’s good. It’s very good.

I approve it. She posts at 6 PM.

By 4:52 PM, before she even posts, I get nervous. The last viral TikTok we did drove 200 orders in 48 hours. We had twelve items in stock. We oversold by 80 units. Mariam and I spent three days calling customers to apologize, offering 15% discounts on future orders, begging them not to post angry comments.

*Thinking: "I should pause the ad campaign before the TikTok drops. But what if the TikTok flops and I paused the ads? I’d lose a whole day of sales. What if it hits and we’re not ready? There’s no playbook for this. Every viral moment is a potential disaster."*

I check inventory one more time. We have 34 units of the linen set. I lower the TikTok coupon from 20% to 10% to slow velocity. Then I message the factory to ask if they can rush a restock. Mahmoud doesn’t reply.

---

## 6:20 PM – Order confirmations with dinner

Mariam left at 5:30. I’m alone in the office now, finishing the COD confirmations we didn’t get to this morning.

The 1,850 EGP order from last night? The customer finally replied. She confirmed. But she wants to change the delivery address to her work in New Cairo. I update it in Shopify, but Bosta already generated the AWB for the old address. Now I have to cancel the AWB, create a new one, and hope the warehouse catches it before the package goes out tomorrow morning.

*Thinking: "This one order has already consumed twenty minutes of my life. We have fifty orders a day. If every order takes twenty minutes of admin, that’s sixteen hours. There are two of us. The math doesn’t work. It never works."*

I eat the cold koshary while I WhatsApp the five customers who still haven’t confirmed their COD orders. Two reply immediately. One asks for a discount. One says she changed her mind. One reads the message and doesn’t reply.

I mark the no-reply as high-risk and hold it. The changed-mind order gets cancelled. That’s another lost sale, but at least I saved the shipping cost.

---

## 8:00 PM – Meta Ads, cash position, and the truth

I finally open my personal bank app. Balance: 23,400 EGP. That’s not the business account — that’s my personal fallback, which I’ve been dipping into for three months to cover ad spend while waiting for COD payouts.

The business account shows 8,700 EGP. But there’s a Meta invoice of 12,800 EGP due in four days. And the factory wants a 50% deposit on the summer drop: 18,000 EGP.

*Thinking: "So I have approximately negative twenty-two thousand EGP in liquid cash. But the Shopify dashboard says we did 89,000 EGP in revenue this month. The disconnect is insane. Revenue is a vanity metric when half your orders are COD and you’re waiting six days to get paid."*

I check the Meta Ads dashboard one more time. Today’s spend: 2,890 EGP. Revenue attributed: 8,100 EGP. ROAS: 2.8×. Below my 3× threshold.

I should pause the campaign. But if I pause it, tomorrow’s order volume drops, and the factory deposit becomes even harder. So I leave it running.

*Thinking: "I’m borrowing from tomorrow to pay for today. Everyone in this market is doing the same thing. The ones who survive are the ones with family money or a B2B side hustle. I have neither."*

---

## 9:45 PM – Finally, the lights go off

I post an Instagram Story showing the packing station with a caption: "Your orders are on their way 💫." It gets 47 views in five minutes. Someone replies: "I ordered yesterday, where is it?"

I reply with a heart emoji and tell myself I’ll check tracking in the morning.

I lock the office. In the Uber home, I scroll through competitor Instagram accounts. Zee launched a new drop today. InYourShoe is running a giveaway. Dresscode posted a Reel that got 12K views. Everyone is grinding. Nobody is sleeping.

*Thinking: "This business is supposed to be about fashion. Design. Creativity. Instead it’s logistics, cash flow, courier negotiations, and begging customers to confirm their phone numbers. I spend 10% of my time on the product and 90% on operations that should be automatic. Something has to give."*

I get home. My mom left dinner in the fridge. I eat it cold while staring at the WhatsApp icon. Seventeen new messages since I left the office.

I read three of them. Then I turn off my phone and go to bed.

## THE DREAM SYSTEM

If I could design the perfect system for my store, here’s what it would do.

### Before the day starts:

- **Overnight COD auto-verification:** Every COD order placed after midnight gets an automated WhatsApp message in Egyptian Arabic: *"Hi [Name], you ordered [Product] for [Amount]. Reply YES to confirm and we’ll ship tomorrow."* If they don’t reply, the order gets flagged. No more morning panic about which orders are real.

- **Inventory truth:** One number, everywhere. The website, the Instagram catalog, the physical stock in the corner, and the TikTok shop all pull from the same count. If someone buys the last medium on Instagram, it disappears from the website instantly. No more overselling.

- **Courier pre-selection:** The system already knows Bosta is faster for Cairo, MylerZ is cheaper for Alexandria, and both charge a surcharge for Minya. It auto-assigns the courier per governorate based on cost, speed, and historical return rate. No more phone calls with Ahmed.

### While I’m slammed during the day:

- **WhatsApp that handles itself:** Sizing questions get an AI reply with our size chart and a recommendation based on weight/height inputs. Order status questions get an instant tracking link. Complaints get routed to me only if the AI can’t solve them in two messages. Mariam’s phone stops melting.

- **Profit-per-order dashboard:** Not ROAS theater. Real numbers. For every order, I see: product cost + shipping cost + COD fee + estimated return probability = actual profit. If an ad campaign is losing money after real costs, the system turns it yellow. Not red — I still decide. But I’m not flying blind.

- **Production reminders tied to drop dates:** When I set a drop date, the system counts backward and pings Mahmoud at T-14, T-7, and T-3 days. If he misses a checkpoint, I get a heads-up to adjust marketing before announcing dates I can’t keep.

### End of day:

- **Cash position, not revenue theater:** A single number showing: confirmed cash in hand + expected COD collections this week - Meta ad bills due - factory deposits = real runway. Not Shopify’s fantasy revenue number.

- **One-tap reconciliation:** Bosta’s payout, MylerZ’s pending transfer, and any cash collected by the driver all feed into one screen. I know what’s real and what’s promised.

- **Return-cost tracking:** Every exchange, every RTO, every fake order gets logged with a cost attached. At the end of the month, I see exactly how much leakage cost me. Not "shipping is expensive." The actual number.

### Old way vs. dream system:

**Old way:**
- I guess which COD orders are real and lose money on bad guesses.
- I manually reconcile three courier dashboards, two payment gateways, and one bank account.
- I don’t know if yesterday was profitable until next week.
- I spend 90% of my time on operations and 10% on the brand.
- Every viral moment is a potential disaster because inventory is a rumor.

**Dream system:**
- I start the day knowing exactly which orders are confirmed and which are fake.
- I see one dashboard that tells me the truth about cash, profit, and inventory.
- I know the real profit of every ad campaign before I scale it.
- I spend 60% of my time on design, marketing, and brand — and 40% on operations that actually need human judgment.
- When a TikTok hits, I’m ready because the system protected me from overselling.

---

*That’s a realistic day in a DTC fashion founder’s shoes in Cairo — and exactly where the right software would change everything.*
